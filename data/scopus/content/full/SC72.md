#### **Abstract** 

Code completion and code repair have become fundamental tasks in software engineering and machine learning research. However, existing large language models (LLMs) for code generation, predominantly based on autoregressive modeling (ARM), exhibit limitations when dealing with incomplete or buggy code snippets, especially when the missing or erroneous spans are located arbitrarily within the sequence. In this paper, we propose **CodeDiffuSe** , a novel masked diffusion framework specifically designed for structure-aware code completion and repair. Unlike traditional ARM approaches, CodeDiffuSe learns to recover missing code spans by leveraging both left and right context, enabling bidirectional reasoning and flexible infilling. We introduce a syntax-aware masking strategy that randomly masks entire Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) subtrees during training, and a semantic consistency regularization that encourages type-correct and syntactically valid predictions. During inference, we propose an error-aware remasking mechanism that dynamically identifies uncertain or invalid tokens and selectively refines them across adaptive reverse diffusion steps. Extensive experiments on standard code completion and bug repair benchmarks, including CodeXGLUE, Defects4J, and QuixBugs, demonstrate that CodeDiffuSe consistently outperforms strong autoregressive baselines such as CodeGen, CodeEditorBench, and InCoder across multiple programming languages. Our work introduces a structure- and semantics-aware diffusion-based alternative for code completion and repair, offering consistent gains in both syntactic validity and functional correctness across diverse benchmarks. Rather than claiming to replace existing paradigms, we demonstrate how diffusion models can complement and improve upon autoregressive and retrieval-based approaches in structure-sensitive settings. 

**Keywords** Code completion · Code repair · Masked diffusion models · Structured code generation · Syntax-aware masking

## **2  Related work** 

Code completion and repair have been historically approached through autoregressive decoding, often using large-scale transformer architectures. Early work such as DeepCoder Balog et al. (2016) and Bayou Allamanis et al. (2017) laid the foundation for neural program synthesis from partial input-output specifications. With the rise of pre-trained large language models (LLMs), OpenAI’s Codex Chen et al. (2021) demonstrated strong performance in code generation from natural language prompts, showcasing the utility of massive code corpora in supervised pretraining. 

CodeGen Nijkamp et al. (2023) and CodeEditorBench Guo et al. (2024) extended these capabilities to code completion and editing. InCoder Fried et al. (2022) introduced infilling with a non-causal attention mechanism, conditioning on both past and future context. CodeT5 Wang et al. (2021) and its improved version CodeT5+ Wang et al. (2023b) provided a unified Seq2Seq framework that supports both code understanding and generation. CodeRL Le et al. (2022) integrated reinforcement learning with code generation to optimize functional correctness. 

Despite their effectiveness, these models rely heavily on leftto-right decoding or causal masking, which introduces limitations when reasoning over bidirectional context or incomplete code segments. Moreover, while retrieval-augmented methods such as ReCode Wang et al. (2023a) and PLBART Ahmad et al. (2021) bring additional information from similar examples, they often fail to generalize well in scenarios where retrieved candidates lack structural alignment with the input. 

Infilling, where models generate missing spans conditioned on both left and right contexts, better mimics realworld software maintenance scenarios. InCoder Fried et al. (2022) and FIM Raychev et al. (2014) introduced infilling objectives within autoregressive training by modifying positional encodings. However, these methods still fundamentally rely on sequence-level token modeling without enforcing structure. 

Defects4J Just et al. (2014) and QuixBugs Lin et al. (2017) provide benchmarks for program repair, leading to neural repair systems like Long and Rinard (2016), DeepFix Gupta et al. (2017). These models leverage code transformations, templates, or fine-tuned LLMs for generating patches. Nonetheless, most lack explicit modeling of structural constraints or semantic correctness during generation, making them prone to syntactic errors or functionally invalid code. 

MLM-based pretraining for code, inspired by BERT Devlin et al. (2019), has been extensively adopted for representation learning. CodeBERT Feng et al. (2020), and 

GraphCodeBERT Guo et al. (2020) apply masked token prediction to learn code embeddings that capture syntactic and semantic relationships. These embeddings have proven useful in downstream tasks such as classification, code search, and clone detection. 

C-BERT Karampatsis and Sutton (2020) and CoTexT Phan et al. (2021 ) extend MLMs with code-specific masking strategies, such as identifier or block-level masking, and multi-task learning. However, MLMs are limited by their non-generative nature and inability to directly reconstruct full sequences, particularly under structured masking or infilling settings. Thus, while useful for encoding, they fall short as generative models for structure-aware completion or repair. 

Diffusion models have become state-of-the-art in continuous domains such as image generation Dhariwal and Nichol (2021). Extensions to discrete domains include Structured Denoising Diffusion Models (SDDMs) Austin et al. (2021 2022). These models ), Diffusion-LM Li et al. ( apply iterative noise addition and denoising to generate discrete sequences and have achieved promising results in natural language generation. 

MaskGIT Chang et al. (2022) proposed parallel denoising with masking schedules for efficient generation. In the context of code, CodeFusion Singh et al. (2023) is a pioneering attempt to apply masked diffusion to code token infilling. While effective in token reconstruction, CodeFusion lacks structural priors such as AST awareness and semantic constraints, and it is limited to short sequences. 

Diffusion modeling for code remains in early stages, and its potential to model program structure and semantics is not yet fully realized. Our work builds on these foundations and introduces ASTguided diffusion with semantic regularization. 

Structured representations of code, such as Abstract Syntax Trees (ASTs), are central to program analysis. Tree2Tree Chen et al. (2018), and grammar-based decoding Rabinovich et al. (2017) encode ASTs for sequence prediction. Graph-based models such as GGNNs Allamanis et al. (2017), ASTNN Zhang et al. (2019), and GraphCodeBERT Guo et al. (2020) incorporate data and control flow into neural encoders. 

However, most of these methods either rely on fully observed code or focus on classification. Their generative components operate autoregressively and do not utilize structural corruption or masked diffusion. Ensuring semantic correctness, including type consistency and compilability, has been addressed by Hellendoorn et al. (2019) and Getafix Bader et al. (2019), yet such properties are rarely embedded into end-to-end generative models. Despite advancements, key limitations remain:

## **3  Methodology** 

In this section, we introduce , a novel _masked_ **CodeDiffuSe** _denoising diffusion_ framework designed specifically for _structure-aware_ code completion and repair. The core idea is to model the conditional distribution of a clean source code sequence given a partially corrupted version, leveraging a diffusionbased generative process that progressively denoises masked or buggy code spans. 

Unlike autoregressive generation, the masked diffusion formulation allows **bidirectional conditioning** over missing code spans, which is crucial for realistic code editing tasks where context appears on both sides of a gap. Furthermore, the use of AST-guided corruption introduces a structured inductive bias, enabling the model to reason over semantically meaningful code fragments (e.g., blocks, functions) rather than flat token sequences. This contrasts with traditional token-noise models and enhances generalization to unseen code structures. 

The framework consists of the following key components: 

- A **syntax-aware masking mechanism** that leverages Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) structures to mask meaningful code fragments during training; 

- A **forward corruption process** that creates masked sequences through progressive subtree-level masking; 

- A **reverse denoising process** that reconstructs missing tokens using a bidirectional Transformerbased decoder; 

- A **semantic consistency regularization** module that enforces syntactic validity and type correctness; 

- An **error-aware remasking strategy** to iteratively refine uncertain or invalid predictions during inference;

### **3.9  Methodological summary and implementation considerations** 

_CodeDiffuSe_ introduces a novel masked diffusion framework tailored for structure-aware code completion and repair. It departs from conventional autoregressive paradigms by enabling bidirectional reasoning through masked denoising over Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) subtrees. The methodology integrates syntax-aware masking, semantic consistency regularization, error-aware remasking, and adaptive inference termination. Together, these components offer a unified framework capable of reconstructing corrupted code spans in a syntax- and type-consistent manner. 

From a theoretical perspective, our model differs from prior text-oriented diffusion approaches by encoding inductive structural biases through AST-aware masking and enforcing functional constraints via semantic regularization. This positions CodeDiffuSe as the first structured denoising diffusion model for program synthesis that explicitly integrates symbolic structure and static correctness checks into its generative dynamics. 

To support reproducibility and practical deployment, we summarize key implementation configurations: 

- **AST Parsing:** Source code is parsed into ASTs using Tree-sitter, which supports major languages including Python, Java, and JavaScript.

### **5.15  Limitations and generalizability** 

**Language Dependence** The model currently relies on language-specific abstract syntax trees (ASTs) and type inference tools. As such, adaptation to new programming languages requires access to reliable parsers and semantic analyzers. Dynamically typed languages (e.g., Python, JavaScript) present further challenges due to the absence of explicit type annotations, potentially limiting the effectiveness of our semantic consistency mechanisms. 

**Functional and Low‑Level Paradigms** Our current design primarily targets imperative, statically typed languages (e.g., Java, C#). Functional programming languages (e.g., Haskell, OCaml) and low-level codebases (e.g., C, embedded code) may require redefinition of structural priors, such as controlflow sensitivity or higher-order AST representations. 

**Training Cost and Diffusion Overhead** Despite recent optimizations, masked diffusion remains computationally expensive compared to autoregressive decoding. While our model achieves better sampling efficiency than earlier discrete diffusion methods, training still requires multiple denoising iterations and AST-guided masking, which may be resourceintensive for industrial-scale deployment. 

**Generalizability** We have evaluated CodeDiffuSe on datasets spanning three programming languages, but further work is needed to test its adaptability to mixed-language codebases, nonEnglish identifiers or comments, and crosslanguage code migration scenarios. Future research could explore incorporating multilingual pretrained embeddings and universal AST representations. 

While CodeDiffuSe introduces promising capabilities, broader adoption will depend on addressing these languagespecific, architectural, and computational challenges. 

To begin with, the evaluation results across multiple benchmarks (Section 5.1) demonstrate that _CodeDiffuSe_ consistently outperforms leading autoregressive and diffusion-based baselines in both surface-level accuracy (e.g., BLEU, Exact Match) and deeper correctness metrics (e.g., AST validity, compilation success, test case accuracy). These improvements validate our core hypothesis (RQ1) that syntax- and semantics-aware masked diffusion can lead to more robust and functionally reliable code generation. 

The ablation studies (Section 5.3 ) offer further insight into the architectural contributions. Syntaxaware masking, which aligns the corruption process with the structural boundaries of code, yields the most substantial gains in syntactic coherence and compilation success. Semantic regularization based on AST and type checking enhances the model’s ability to produce type-safe and parsable outputs, while remasking and adaptive step control improve denoising stability and computational efficiency. These findings affirm the effectiveness of each component in contributing to overall model performance (RQ2), and clarify how the integration of symbolic constraints into the generative process provides concrete advantages. 

With regard to efficiency (RQ3), our convergence analysis (Section 5.4 ) reveals that CodeDiffuSe achieves comparable or superior accuracy with significantly fewer reverse diffusion steps compared to fixed-step alternatives, thus enabling faster inference without compromising quality. This is particularly relevant for real-world applications where latency is a practical constraint. 

The qualitative analysis (Section 5.5) highlights the model’s strengths in preserving block-level syntactic structures and ensuring semantic alignment across infilled spans. CodeDiffuSe demonstrates resilience in scenarios involving complex edits, nested structures, and variable renaming, confirming its robustness under semantically neutral perturbations (RQ4, RQ5). Furthermore, the model generalizes effectively across different programming paradigms— object-oriented, procedural, and functional—as shown in our cross-paradigm evaluation (Section 5.7), addressing RQ6.
