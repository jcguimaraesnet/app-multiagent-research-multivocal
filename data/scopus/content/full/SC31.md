#### **1. Introduction** 

ECs have emerged as a promising organizational model to harness distributed energy resources, promote self-consumption and empower end-users to actively participate in energy markets [1]. Within this context, LEMs provide mechanisms for community members (residential, commercial and industrial consumers and prosumers) to share and trade energy among themselves, optimizing local resources and enhancing flexibility [2]. To address the complexities inherent in managing such systems, our research aims to design a framework that enables agent-based simulation and modelling of the ECs and its members using a modular and extensible framework that integrates detailed modelling of community-level energy consumption and production with 

an agent-based market simulation for peer-to-peer (P2P) trading. The framework contains multiple models divided into two main categories: i) EC models define the members and their assets (appliances, photovoltaic (PV) systems, storage devices); ii) LEM models define the trading mechanisms and Business Models (BMs) for LEM. 

The _EC models_ capture the diversity of household behaviors, asset ownership and weather-driven renewable generation, enabling highresolution simulations of energy flows within the community. Since the smart meters may not cover 100 % of the households or their measurements are available ex-post, a model for the baseline load estimation is proposed. The algorithm adopts a heuristic modelling approach, combining domain-driven rules with parameter optimization to estimate individual household load curves from contextual and aggregated data.

It utilizes historical smart meter data when available or aggregated hourly monthly load profiles otherwise. The process begins by deriving a normalized community-wide load pattern that captures typical load behavior acting as a temporal weighting factor. For each member, a parametric baseline model is built using weather-dependent factors and household-specific characteristics (occupancy, house size, number of appliances). Model parameters are optimized by minimizing the loss function of the estimated and actual loads. The proposed algorithm outputs the estimated time series load curves for each household. 

The _LEM models_ implement an agent-based trading architecture, where each member of the community is represented as a market agent capable of generating bids or offers and participating in dynamic market clearing. A new pricing mechanism is proposed to overcome the disadvantages of the Uniform Price (UP), dynamically adjusting the prices of the remaining (unmatched) bids to create a new intersection of demand and supply curves, maximizing the total traded quantity on LEM. 

An important feature of the framework is its extensibility. Recognizing the evolving nature of ECs, the framework has been designed to support the integration of new BMs that reflect emerging trends in distributed generation, electric vehicle (EV) integration, battery storage, flexibility services and aggregator-driven participation in wholesale markets. To facilitate this, a Generator for EC Code snippets ( _GenECodes_ ) is designed and implemented using LLMs [3], [4] that generates the code for modelling new electrical assets or BMs with production-ready capabilities, ensuring seamless integration with the existing framework. _GenECodes_ is designed on a three-layer architecture, using a template-driven approach with predictive pattern correction and validation layers that automatically identify, correct and validate the code. The framework models and its extensibility capabilities enable rapid prototyping for testing and evaluating the impact of new BMs on the members of the EC, analyzing the techno-economic implications of different market mechanisms and regulatory designs. 

Our research showcases the design and implementation of the framework, detailing its core models, class structures and simulations pipelines. The methodology describes the EC and LEM models, their interaction and the architecture of _GenECodes_ by which new models and BMs are integrated using LLMs and template-driven approach. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. 2 reviews the relevant literature on EC business models, modelling approaches and local market mechanisms. 3 presents the proposed methodology, including the EC modelling framework, asset operation algorithms, the DAUP pricing mechanism and the GenECodes generative architecture. 4 reports the simulation setup, validation procedures and case study results. 5 discusses the findings, computational considerations and limitations. Finally, 6 concludes the paper and outlines directions for future research. 

#### **2. Literature review** 

During the European energy transition, interest in ECs grew significantly. To encourage broader adoption, researchers emphasized the need to explore them through a BM lens. Therefore, [5] examined 90 cases of ECs and innovative companies, identifying 25 distinct BM design options. Although the recent formal recognition of ECs within EU legislation, existing research on their BMs remained fragmented and lacked clear categorization. Thus, [6] set out to deliver a structured analysis of both established and emerging BMs, with a focus on the value they deliver. Drawing on case studies from various European countries, the research identified eight archetypes of EC BMs, shaped by the current regulatory environment. The findings pointed to the need for further exploration of innovative models to help solidify the role of ECs in the energy transition and inform future regulatory progress. 

In the context of France’s traditionally centralized energy sector, [7] examined five distinct EC BMs and assessed their alignment with four policy objectives: expanding RES capacity, attracting private investment, offering grid flexibility and empowering consumers. The analysis 

revealed that none of the EC BMs currently contribute to grid flexibility and the research recommended ensuring economic viability, strengthening intermediary actors and involving local governments as facilitators. 

Moreover, ECs have gained prominence as collaborative entities in Europe’s evolving energy sector, introducing BMs that aim to generate a wide spectrum of value beyond economic metrics. Despite this recognition, the ways in which these varied forms of value emerge remain underexplored. Thus, [8] tackled the issue by first framing ECs within the broader historical and academic discourse on community engagement in energy systems. It then applied concepts from BM theory to differentiate between the processes that enable value generation and the specific outcomes that result. Furthermore, EC initiatives aim to promote citizen involvement and fairness in the energy transition, but often fall short in addressing distributive justice, risking the reinforcement of income inequalities [9]. Focusing on the Netherlands and using a mixed-methods approach, the analysis revealed that while these initiatives reflect some justice principles, few were designed to include or benefit low-income groups, underscoring the need for more inclusive BMs. Additionally, [10] explored how EC BMs in England adapted to this shifting policy context, focusing specifically on PV projects. It identified three main BM types that emerged in succession and examined their development, underlying drivers and the benefits delivered to ECs. 

Additionally, [11] identified the factors influencing BMs for residential energy sharing and, through simulation, evaluated their feasibility from both the aggregator’s and users’ perspectives, including the minimum viable size for a renewable EC. Moreover, [12] reviewed the current state of EC modelling, emphasizing the integration of BMs and defined objectives. Qualitative analysis also explored interdisciplinary and international collaborations. Findings revealed notable research gaps, particularly in linking technical design, policy and economics, especially in multi-energy and sector-coupled systems, which remain underexplored. 

The rise of P2P trading, collective self-consumption and transactive energy has led to new BM structures in LEMs. While much research has focused on the macroeconomic transition of the energy system, limited attention has been given to the microeconomic roles of individual actors. Addressing this gap, [13] presented a systematic review of 135 peer-reviewed articles, analyzing 221 businesses to identify nine macro-actor categories. For each, a representative BM archetype was developed using the BM canvas. Also, [14] aimed to identify measures to enhance ECs’ diffusion and integration by examining the relationships between ECs and stakeholders, along with their respective roles and opportunities. The analysis revealed several barriers and highlighted the need for stronger collaboration between ECs and traditional actors such as energy suppliers, service providers, aggregators and distribution system operators (DSOs). Furthermore, [15] developed an integrated approach based on a linear bottom-up optimization model to address several aspects of ECs: (a) optimal dispatch and technology mix, (b) the impact of Demand Side Management (DSM) and (c) a fair benefit-sharing method supported by a newly proposed fairness index. The model was applied to a case study within the Italian regulatory context. Results showed that including DSM and a diverse EC composition significantly reduced investment needs, by 13 % for PV and 93 % for storage, compared to a scenario without DSM. In more uniform ECs, – RES usage dropped by 20 33 %, and bill savings by about 30 %. 

Another research introduced a conceptual framework to classify energy storage BMs and distinguish between different investment pathways [16]. The framework was then applied to evaluate which storage technologies align with each BM, supported by a review of recent literature on profitability. However, many potentially viable combinations remained underexplored, highlighting the need for more comprehensive research to fully understand storage profitability. Also, [17] focused on Demand Response (DR) BMs as a sustainable solution to enhance flexibility. The research developed an analytical framework to assess DR BMs through the lenses of value proposition, value creation

Although several agent-based and data-driven models for ECs exist, current simulation frameworks face the following limitations: i) rely on manual implementation of household assets, flexibility models and 

market mechanisms; ii) lack automated or generative capabilities for creating new models; iii) are not designed to support rapid scenario generation, template-based extensibility, or market-level experimentation in tariff-constrained LEMs. Moreover, no existing EC simulators integrate generative AI capabilities for synthesizing, validating and correcting code in a structured and reproducible manner. Considering the notable scarcity of EC and BM software design, our research presents a modular and extensible simulation framework designed to model the operation of ECs and their participation in LEMs through agent-based modeling and weather-driven asset simulation. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

- A heuristic-based algorithm for estimating household electricity consumption, combining contextual parameters (weather, occupancy, appliance ownership) with deterministic consumption signatures and historical data. This enables reliable household load profile generation even under partial data availability, while preserving aggregate consistency and mitigating identifiability issues. 

- A new market-clearing mechanism, Dynamically Adjusted Uniform Price (DAUP), which overcomes the limitations of traditional uniform pricing by iteratively adjusting unmatched bids within tariff bounds. DAUP maximizes traded quantities and improves local energy utilization while ensuring convergence, boundedness and compatibility with regulatory constraints, an aspect not addressed in existing EC market models. 

- The design and implementation of _GenECodes_ , a generative code module powered by LLMs that introduces a three-layer architecture (template-driven prompting, predictive pattern correction and physics-aware functional validation) which ensures that automatically generated energy asset models and pricing mechanisms are structurally correct, semantically consistent and directly integrable into the framework. This goes beyond simple software templating by embedding algorithmic safeguards, error-typology-based correction logic and automated test-suite verification, filling a key methodological gap in current EC simulation tools. 

#### **3. Methodology** 

The proposed framework aims to provide a set of models for simulating the operation of ECs based on weather data, structure, number and type of members and their assets. The framework also includes models for trading the demand or surplus on LEMs using different market clearing and settlement mechanisms. To extend the capabilities of the framework, a generative model for code snippets, _GenECodes_ , is proposed. It leverages a three-layer architecture to generate productionready energy community models using LLMs. It relies on a templatebased generation, where structured prompts embed immutable framework patterns, algorithmic pseudocode and integration specifications to guide consistent code creation. This is followed by a predictive pattern correction layer that applies rule-based corrections to common generation errors, including missing methods, incorrect imports and deprecated operations. The final validation layer performs structural, runtime and performance testing through iterative correction cycles. The generated code snippets are automatically integrated into the proposed framework for seamless connection with the existing core modules. 

The methodology illustrated in Fig. 1 is designed on four distinct steps as follows: 

_Step 1. Initializes the EC structure_ : number and type of members (residential, commercial, public), their load profiles, house characteristics and occupancy. For each member of the community, a set of assets is added, their operating model being defined in the core framework. In case an asset is not included in the framework, it can be added by _GenECodes_ using a predefined template that specifies its attributes and operating steps. 

_Step 2. Simulates the operation of the EC based on weather data_ extracted from an API. The weather data span a historical period of 90

#### _3.1. Step 1. structural modelling of the EC environment_ 

The EC is defined using three main classes: EnergyCommunity, Member and Asset in a hierarchical order (as in Figure A1 in Annex 2), modelling EC characteristics, member type (households or commercial/ public units), profiles and their energy assets such as appliances, EV ownership and RES generators. At the top level, the EnergyCommunity class encapsulates geographic data (latitude and longitude), Time-OfUse tariffs (TOU), Feed-In Tariffs (FIT) and local weather information, which are the inputs for simulating the operation of the community. This class maintains a list of Member instances, representing individual participants within the community and aggregates their operational states and economic metrics over time. 

Each Member class represents a household or building with detailed socio-technical attributes, including household size, occupancy, appliances and heating/cooling systems. These attributes influence the member’ s load profiles, which are generated using the create_member_states() method by combining occupancy patterns with weather data. Members may own a variety of energy assets, such as PV systems, wind turbines (WT) and batteries. The simulate_assets_operation() method orchestrates the simulation of these assets’ operation based on weather conditions, resulting in time-series data for consumption and generation. Cost and revenue calculations are performed at the member level through the calculate_hourly_costs() method, which considers TOU and FIT tariffs to estimate expenditures and payments. The EnergyCommunity class aggregates these results across all members to compute community-level states, enabling analysis of the community’s overall energy balance and economic performance. 

Energy assets within the EC model are organized into a hierarchy, with an abstract Asset class defining common attributes, methods and subclasses like BaselineLoad, PVAsset, WindAsset and BatteryAsset implementing specific operation logic. These classes produce time-series data capturing consumption, generation and storage dynamics at the asset level, which are then aggregated at the member and EC levels. The operate()method enables two types of operation: simulation-based operation using weather data and measurement-based operation using different types of communication interfaces (API or MQTT stream). 

As depicted in the sequence diagram in Figure A2 (Annex 2), in the first step, the user initializes EnergyCommunity with its geographic coordinates and tariff settings. Members are added to the EC, each configured with household attributes and a portfolio of energy assets. In case an asset is not in the core framework, it is generated using _GenECodes_ based on a predefined asset template.

For obtaining the load curves, the proposed algorithm builds a data-driven estimation model for electricity consumption using both contextual parameters and historical measurements. The proposed algorithm does not aim to uniquely reconstruct each household’s actual load from aggregate data, as this would be unidentifiable without individual measurements. Instead, the method produces representative synthetic load profiles that remain consistent with typical residential consumption patterns and with each household’s parametrized load structure. #### _3.2.2. PV asset_

#### _3.2.3. WT asset_ 

In the case of the Wind Turbine (WT), the output power is computed using a deterministic parametric model based on the turbine’s hub height, rated power and wind-speed characteristics. The model considers the wind-speed profile at hub height, calculated from measured or API-derived wind data using the power-law relationship with exponent _α_ . The aerodynamic and operational characteristics of the turbine are defined by three key parameters: cut-in wind speed ( _WS_ in), rated wind speed ( _WS_ rated) and cut-out wind speed ( _WS_ out). The output power follows a standard piecewise formulation widely used in small and medium wind-turbine modeling [38]: i) below cut-in speed the WT produces no power; ii) between cut-in and rated speed, the output increases according to the cubic wind-power law, scaled to the rated capacity; iii) between rated and cut-out speed, the turbine delivers its rated power; iv) above cut-out speed, the turbine shuts down for safety, and output returns to zero. Algorithm A1 in Annex 3 models the output power based on this deterministic model.

#### _3.2.5. Adding new assets using GenECodes_ 

In case other assets are needed to shape the consumption of the members or EC, they can be added to the framework by the proposed generative model for code snippets. The foundation of the model rests upon a template-based approach that bridges domain expertise with LLM capabilities. The template represents a structured prompt containing predefined code scaffolding, parameter placeholders and algorithmic specifications that guide language model generation toward framework-compliant implementations. The template serves as a comprehensive specification document that includes asset metadata, such as name, type and description, base class structure with inheritance patterns and method signatures, parameter initialization sections with type specifications and default values, and pseudocode implementations translated from domain expertise. 

The template mechanism operates through systematic placeholder replacement where asset-specific configurations populate predefined slots within the structured prompt. Each template contains immutable framework patterns presented as executable code blocks rather than abstract descriptions, ensuring consistent integration points across all generated assets. The template structure includes mandatory sections for class definition, constructor patterns, simulation method frameworks and plotting capabilities, providing comprehensive scaffolding that eliminates interpretation ambiguity while maintaining implementation flexibility for physics-specific algorithms. 

The template-driven approach fundamentally differs from traditional prompt engineering by providing concrete structural requirements embedded within the generation context. _GenECodes_ model employs a systematic _three-layer architecture_ that addresses the fundamental limitations observed in basic template-driven approaches. The initial tests on a template-driven model achieved only 35–45 % success rates due to its reliance on reactive error correction and descriptive guidance that left substantial interpretation variance for LLM. Analysis of generation failures revealed recurring patterns including incorrect import statements, missing framework integration calls, improper method signatures and deprecated operations, requiring manual intervention in more than 50 % of cases. Therefore, the threelayer architecture is considered in the proposed _GenECodes_ model (Fig. 2): the first layer provides enhanced template generation with prescriptive framework patterns embedded as executable code blocks as shown in Annex4; the second layer applies predictive pattern correction based on empirically observed failure modes; the third layer performs validation with automatic remediation capabilities. 

_Layer 1_ -template generation reconceptualizes prompt engineering by embedding complete framework patterns as immutable requirements within generation prompts. Each template contains distinct sections guiding LLM behavior: i) the mandatory framework patterns sections includes exact code snippets for class structure, method signatures, and framework integration calls, explicitly providing the simulate operation method structure with required calls to super create asset states and super update asset states; ii) the physics implementation specifications translate domain expertise into structured pseudocode with detailed algorithmic guidance, specifying computational steps (e.g., in case of a Heat Pump (HP)-heating demand calculations based on temperature differentials and thermal properties, coefficient of performance applications for electrical consumption determination, and thermal dynamics for temperature updates); iii) the integration requirements section standardizes data flow patterns, variable naming conventions, and return value structures across all generated assets, ensuring interoperability between automatically generated and manually developed components. 

_Layer 2_ -the predictive pattern correction layer systematically

addresses known failure modes through rule-based modifications operating deterministically on established patterns. The correction layer contains four primary sections addressing syntax fixes for common transcription errors, missing pattern injection when required framework patterns are absent, parameter initialization correction for undefined attributes and operation updates replacing deprecated functionality. The corrector maintains comprehensive mappings of systematic errors and applies corrections automatically, addressing patterns occurring in 65–90 % of generation attempts. Missing pattern injection represents the most complex aspect, inserting required framework patterns at appropriate code locations while considering context and maintaining logical flow. To avoid reinforcing structural errors produced in the first layer, this layer follows a validation-first strategy. Before attempting any modifications, it evaluates the generated code against a set of structural invariants defined by the framework, including required classes, inheritance rules, method signatures and data-exchange formats. If these checks fail, the correction layer does not refine or extend the faulty structure; instead, it regenerates the missing or incorrect components using canonical templates that enforce the correct architecture. Only when the structural form is valid does the layer apply targeted fixes, such as resolving undefined variables or adjusting boundary conditions. It prevents error propagation across layers and ensures that incomplete or incorrect code structures are overwritten rather than reinforced. 

_Layer 3_ -the validation layer provides comprehensive testing with automatic issue resolution through multiple validation phases. Structural validation examines the generated code for required methods, proper inheritance relationships and framework integration calls. #### _3.3. Step 3. trading on LEM_ 

To enable P2P trading, the LEM model depicted in the class diagram in Figure A4 (Annex 2) is implemented using an agent-based model. The LEMCommunity class manages the market simulation, orchestrating the time-stepped activation of individual agents and the execution of market clearing process. It uses the EnergyCommunity to access members’ net balances and tariff structures. Each market participant is modelled as a LEMAgent class, which represents an Agent class of the Mesa<sup>2</sup> Python package encapsulates the trading behavior of an EC member. The LEMAgent class includes methods to generate buy or sell offers based on the member’s net balance at each time step, using a randomized pricing strategy bounded by the community’s TOU and FIT tariffs. These offers are submitted to the LEMCommunity that uses an external market 

clearing engine, implemented using the PyMarket<sup>3</sup> Python package. The LEMCommunity is initialized with the EC structure and a time window for the simulation (as in Figure A5 in Annex 2). At each time step, LEMAgents are instantiated to represent the market behavior of each member. Based on their net energy balances, agents generate buy or sell offers to the market. In the event of surplus within the EC, the market performs a clearing process to allocate trades between buyers and sellers. Following the execution of trades, each agent updates its internal state based on the results. Upon completion of the simulation, all market outcomes are consolidated to deliver a holistic view of the EC’s trading activities. 

The market clearing process can be performed using different pricing mechanisms such as UP, Pay-as-Bid (PaB) or other pricing mechanisms [39], [40]. The framework encapsulates the UP mechanism and a proposed adjusted UP but enables new pricing mechanisms to be generated using _GenECodes_ . 

#### _3.3.1. UP mechanism_ 

The UP mechanism clears LEM trades using a UP rule, where all transactions occur at a single market-clearing price set by the intersection of aggregated demand and supply curves. The algorithm follows the steps: 

i) It constructs demand ( _D_ ) and supply ( _S_ ) curves by sorting bids ( **B** ): buyers by decreasing price, sellers by increasing price. 

ii) Using PyMarket method _bids.intersect stepwise_ ( _D, S_ ), it identifies the point where total demand meets total supply. This intersection determines the clearing price ( _p_<sup>∗</sup> ), quantity ( _q_<sup>∗</sup> ) and the biddings that can be accepted for buying and selling ( _b_<sup>∗</sup> _, s_<sup>∗</sup> ). Only these bids are involved up to the intersection point and are retained for trading. 

iii) Build market sides to compute transactions: the short side (fewer total units) is fully cleared at the clearing price and the long side is partially cleared up to the traded quantity. Transactions ( **T** ) are generated for all bids on the short side and proportionally for those on the long side, all at the same UP. Therefore, all accepted trades occur at price _p_<sup>∗</sup> , up to the matched quantity _q_<sup>∗</sup> . The pseudo-code for market clearing using UP is depicted in Algorithm A3 in Annex 3. The main advantage of the UP is its simplicity and transparency, which enhances market fairness and participant confidence. By settling all accepted bids at a single clearing price, it ensures non-discriminatory treatment: all buyers pay the same price, and all sellers receive the same price for traded energy, regardless of their individual bid prices. On the other hand, the main disadvantage of the UP lies in its potential to encourage strategic bidding behavior. Since all accepted participants receive the same clearing price regardless of their individual bid prices, participants might be incentivized to bid untruthfully to influence the market clearing price in their favor. For example, a seller might submit a higher price than their true marginal cost, hoping that the UP will still clear above their bid, reducing overall market efficiency and leading to prices that do not reflect actual supply and demand conditions. Additionally, the UP rule does not guarantee that all available quantity is traded since only the bids involved up to the intersection point are retained for trading.

For EVs, the suite checks daily charging energy consistency, maximum charging power constraints and logical adherence to the defined charging window. For each asset, we report the percentage of tests passed (i) on the first attempt, (ii) after automatic correction by the predictive correction layer, and (iii) after any minor manual refinements if needed. Second, we perform scenario-based performance testing across a grid of operating conditions. HPs are evaluated under varying combinations of outdoor temperature, insulation level, occupancy and HVAC set points; EVs under different daily travel distances, charging windows and station power levels; and batteries under a range of surplus/deficit profiles. For each scenario, we assess whether the generated model produced logically consistent behavior (e.g., monotonic heating demand with increasing temperature differentials, feasible charging curves) and respected operational constraints. Third, we quantify constraint- 

violation rates, including violations of thermal limits, SOC bounds, maximum power constraints and energy-balance consistency. These violations remain rare after correction, confirming that the LLMgenerated code behaves robustly under diverse scenarios. Since no real-world ground-truth datasets exist for the newly generated assets, these functional and scenario-based tests provide an objective and domain-consistent method for evaluating semantic correctness.

#### **5. Discussion** 

The computational complexity of the core components of the proposed framework is predominantly linear, ensuring scalability for largescale simulations of ECs. The deterministic asset operating models for baseline load, PV, wind and battery assets operate through a single pass over all members and simulation steps. For _n_ members and Δ _T_ hourly intervals, each asset evaluates constant-time physics or rule-based functions, leading to an overall complexity of **O** ( _n_ Δ _T_ ). This linear scaling supports efficient execution over multi-month horizons and for communities with hundreds of participants. In the case of DAUP, each iteration performs a market-clearing step equivalent to the UP mechanism, which requires sorting bids with complexity **O** ( **B** log **B** )for **B** submitted offers. With a small number of adjustment iterations (typically between 2 and 6 due to rapid convergence), the total perinterval complexity becomes **O** ( _K_ **B** log **B** ). The GenECodes generative module contributes minimal computational overhead to the simulation process. Template-based generation and predictive correction operate in time proportional to the generated code length ( _L_ ), so the complexity is **O** ( _L_ ). Validation steps (structural checks and lightweight runtime tests) add **O** ( _L_ )and **O** ( _T_ )operations but are performed only once during model creation, not during the operational simulation. Consequently, GenECodes enables rapid and automated integration of new assets and pricing mechanisms without affecting the runtime scaling of the broader framework.

As the number of members _n_ , trading agents and market bids **B** increases, memory usage and per-interval processing times grow proportionally, and the DAUP mechanism, although efficient, may require more adjustment cycles in highly unbalanced markets. Similarly, adding multiple flexible assets (e.g., EV fleets, HP with dynamic control) increases the density of state updates and interdependencies, potentially leading to higher simulation overhead. These scalability constraints do not hinder performance for typical EC sizes (typically a few hundred members), but they highlight the need for future extensions such as parallel execution, distributed market clearing or model-order reduction techniques to maintain efficiency for very large-scale or real-time applications. 

Beyond computational scalability, several methodological limitations should be acknowledged. Thus, the deterministic asset models (PV, wind, battery, baseline load, HP, EV) rely on physics-based or rule-based formulations and do not capture stochastic variability such as abrupt behavioral shifts, rapid weather deviations or atypical consumption events, which may reduce accuracy under extreme or highly volatile conditions. Also, the _GenECodes_ framework, while effective for automating code generation, depends on the correctness and completeness of the predefined templates, meaning that poorly specified templates or missing domain constraints may propagate systematic modelling errors. 

The framework currently represents user behavior (e.g., EV charging times, heating setpoints) using rule-based assumptions, without explicitly modelling higher-level behavioral dynamics such as preferencedriven decision-making, comfort-based strategies or learning-based scheduling policies. Future work will address these limitations through probabilistic asset models, strategic agent-based modelling, reinforcement learning driven behavior simulation and more rigorous validation against diverse real-world datasets. 

Although several tools exist for modelling distributed energy systems: such as HOMER Energy for techno-economic sizing, OpenDSS and PyPSA for power-flow simulations and EnergyPlus for thermal and building modelling, direct comparison with our framework is challenging due to fundamental differences in scope and design. These tools focus on specific subsystems and do not provide integrated agent-based LEM simulation, dynamic pricing mechanisms or automated model generation. Likewise, existing agent-based EC simulators require manual coding of new assets, lack weather-driven deterministic asset models or do not support tariff-bounded pricing. In contrast, our framework combines asset-level deterministic simulation, LEM clearing through UP and DAUP mechanisms and a generative module (GenECodes) capable of producing new asset models and pricing mechanisms automatically. Because no existing tool offers this combination of modelling depth, market integration and automated extensibility, establishing a direct benchmark is non-trivial. 

#### **6. Conclusions** 

This paper presents an extensible simulation framework tailored to the modelling of ECs and LEM participation. The framework integrates load and generation models, agent-based market simulation, and automated asset and pricing mechanism generation, offering a robust platform for analyzing techno-economic performance under diverse scenarios. 

Three contributions are included in the methodology. First, a heuristic, data-driven baseline load estimation algorithm is proposed, combining contextual variables (such as weather and household 

attributes) with partial historical data to accurately reconstruct member-level consumption profiles. Second, a novel market-clearing mechanism, the DAUP, is introduced to overcome the inefficiencies of the UP. The proposed mechanism dynamically shifts bids to maximize traded quantities, resulting in significant improvements in surplus utilization, self-consumption and cost savings. Third, the framework implements _GenECodes_ , a generative code model powered by LLMs, which enables the automatic generation of new asset models and pricing mechanisms through a three-layer architecture: template-based generation, predictive correction and multi-stage validation. 

To reduce hallucination, _GenECodes_ incorporates a multi-stage control pipeline that constrains the LLM’s output. First, template-based prompts embed immutable framework patterns, preventing the model from inventing structures, classes or APIs that are not present in the simulation environment. Second, a predictive correction layer systematically removes common hallucinations (such as undefined variables, non-existent methods or incorrect import paths) based on recurring error signatures observed during preliminary experiments. Third, a strict validation layer (structural, runtime, integration and performance tests) filters out semantically incorrect code. Thus, the multi-layered approach ensures the LLM never directly injects unverified or unchecked code into the framework, reducing hallucination and enforcing determinism in the generation process. 

The technological implementation of _GenECodes_ leverages GPT-3.5turbo as the primary LLM engine, selected through comparative benchmarking against alternative models including Ollama-hosted open-source variants such as CodeLlama. The selection is validated through extensive performance analysis revealing that GPT-3.5-turbo with the integration system achieves first-attempt success rates of 71–82 % compared to the original approach with 35–45 %. _GenECodes_ significantly reduces development time while ensuring code reliability, framework compliance and seamless integration. 

The framework is validated through simulations on a real EC, con=0.98 for baseline firming high accuracy of the asset models (e.g., R<sup>2</sup> load, R<sup>2</sup> =0.93 for PV output), and demonstrating that DAUP improves traded quantities, reduces grid dependency and increases community _GenECodes_ self-sufficiency. achieved first-attempt success rates above 70 % for complex asset models like HPs and EV chargers. Generated models undergo rigorous validation to ensure both technical correctness and interoperability. The validation process includes structural verification to confirm proper implementation of required methods and integration points and algorithmic testing using diverse scenarios to verify correct behavior. The framework’s modular architecture enables the integration of newly generated models without requiring modifications to the broader simulation infrastructure. 

The newly introduced DAUP mechanism significantly improved market performance: traded energy increased from 2508 kWh to 3199 kWh (+27 %), the Self-Consumption Index rose from 42 % to 96 %, and the Self-Sufficiency Index from 17 % to 38 %, while the Grid Dependence Index decreased from 66.3 % to 61.6 %. These numerical results confirm the reliability of the framework and the practical value of DAUP and GenECodes in enhancing EC efficiency and extensibility. 

The results underscore the importance of enabling flexible, digitalready simulation environments to support the rapid testing and deployment of emerging BMs in the context of decentralized energy systems. By automating assets and market mechanisms’ integration, the
