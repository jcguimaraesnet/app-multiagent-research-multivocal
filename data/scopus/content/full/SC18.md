# A Preference-Driven Methodology for Efficient Code Generation 

Yuqi Li , _Member, IEEE_ , Zijie Zhou , Zhiyuan Peng , Junhao Dong , _Graduate Student Member, IEEE_ , Haochen You , Renye Yan , Shiping Wen , Yingli Tian , _Fellow, IEEE_ , and Tingwen Huang , _Fellow, IEEE_ 

**_Abstract_ —With the rise of large language models (LLMs), significant progress has been made in research related to code automation within the field of software engineering. Among these developments, the code generation task has attracted considerable attention from both academia and industry due to its broad applicability. However, most existing studies focus primarily on improving the correctness of the code generated by LLMs, while overlooking the efficiency attributes of the code. As a result, LLMs often produce correct code without considering human preferences for efficiency. Efficient code not only conserves resources but also enhances the user experience. For online optimization scenarios, we find that the fixed clipping mechanism of group relative policy optimization (GRPO) can lead to** **_entropy collapse_ , causing premature convergence to suboptimal solutions. To address this, we propose DC-GRPO. The method employs a multilevel reward that accounts for compilation success, functional correctness, and execution efficiency to alleviate sparse-reward issues. In addition, a dynamic** **_clipping_ mechanism based on a four-quadrant analysis of advantage and generation probability adapts the clipping bounds via a nonlinear mapping, encouraging low-probability/high-reward responses while suppressing high-probability/low-reward ones. In our experiments on a 1.5B-parameter code model, DC-GRPO achieves an eff@5 of 13.4%, an improvement of 1.2% points over standard GRPO, and shows consistent gains over other baselines on code-oriented LLMs.** 

**_Impact Statement_ —DC-GRPO advances LLM-based code generation by optimizing for execution efficiency alongside correctness, addressing a key limitation in current methods. ## I. INTRODUCTION 

**I** N the domain of code generationtion efficiency of code generated by large language models [1], enhancing the execu(LLMs) remains a critical challenge [2], [3]. Traditional offline reinforcement learning (RL) methods, such as direct preference optimization (DPO), rely on static datasets to optimize policies. While this approach reduces interaction costs, it suffers from several limitations: the training signal is constrained by the predefined data distribution, implicitly assuming that the training dataset conforms to an optimal policy distribution. Consequently, the ability to actively explore superior code implementations is restricted, leading to diminished generalization capabilities when encountering unseen problem patterns and even policy degradation in the face of data distribution shifts. In the context of code efficiency optimization, offline RL methods struggle to cover dynamically changing and complex logical structures; the generated code is often confined to patterns existing within the dataset and lacks deep optimization for execution efficiency. 

In contrast, online RL does not require preprepared training data and can guide the model to generate outputs more closely aligned with human preferences in real-time through a reward model. The core of online RL lies in establishing a continuous learning loop within a dynamic environment: the agent generates trajectory data through real-time interactions and immediately utilizes environmental feedback for policy optimization. This iterative “explore-exploit-update” mechanism endows the algorithm with strong environmental adaptability. Among these methods, group relative policy optimization (GRPO) emerges as an ideal framework for efficient fine-tuning. It discards the value network of traditional proximal policy optimization (PPO) and instead employs a group sampling and relative advantage estimation mechanism. By generating multiple candidate responses and calculating their relative advantages, GRPO significantly enhances training stability and resource utilization. It demonstrates unique strengths, especially in multiobjective tasks like code efficiency optimization, which require a comprehensive evaluation of compilation success rates, functional correctness, and runtime performance. 

However, in their experiments applying GRPO, Yu et al. [4] discovered that the entropy of the model’s generation policy rapidly decreases as training progresses. This indicates that the model ceases exploration prematurely and tends to generate nearly identical responses. The likely cause of this phenomenon is the rigid policy updates resulting from the fixed clipping mechanism in standard GRPO: there is insufficient incentive to explore high-reward, low-probability responses (e.g., breakthrough optimization solutions), while the corrective force on inefficient, high-probability responses remains limited. This constrains the model’s performance ceiling in code efficiency optimization. In other words, while the clipping setting in GRPO can prevent the policy from deviating excessively from the reference model, it also hinders necessary probability corrections for certain policies. This leads to the model generating repetitive responses and triggers entropy collapse. 

In typical applications of GRPO, the clipping hyperparameter _ϵ_ is generally set to 0.2. In the quantitative analysis of sequence probability changes described above, the update for any given sequence—whether of high or low probability—depends on its current probability and its advantage value. From a qualitative standpoint, applying the same fixed clipping coefficient to sequences with 

different advantage values and generation probabilities fails to effectively balance the exploration of high-quality policies and the elimination of low-quality ones. Therefore, designing an algorithmic framework that dynamically adjusts the clipping threshold based on advantage values and confidence is of great significance for enhancing the ability of large models to generate efficient code. Our work makes three primary advances. 

1. _Algorithmic innovation_ : We propose dynamic clip GRPO (DC-GRPO), the first RL framework to address entropy collapse in code efficiency optimization. By dynamically adjusting clipping thresholds based on a four-quadrant analysis of advantage values and generation probabilities, DC-GRPO promotes exploration of high-reward, lowprobability code solutions while suppressing inefficient high-probability outputs. 

2. _Holistic reward design_ : We introduce a multilevel reward function unifying compilation success (binary), functional correctness (test-case-pass rate), and execution efficiency (normalized runtime), enabling joint optimization of these critical dimensions without sparse reward penalties. 

3. _Empirical advancements_ : Experiments on the APPS benchmark show DC-GRPO outperforms GRPO by 1.2% in eff@5 on 1.5B-parameter models, with ablation studies validating the necessity of both dynamic clipping components (probability and advantage) and optimal hyperparameter configurations. 

To the best of our knowledge, DC-GRPO is the first method to integrate dynamic clipping with group-relative policy optimization, offering a scalable solution for aligning LLMgenerated code with human preferences for efficiency. 

## II. RELATED WORK 

## _A. Large Language Model_ 

The proliferation of natural language processing has catalyzed the development of LLMs [5], which have achieved widespread adoption. Their success is largely attributed to their scalability, allowing them to be trained with billions of parameters on vast datasets, which results in significant performance enhancements. LLMs can be adapted for downstream applications through two primary methodologies: fine-tuning [6], [7], [8] and prompting [9], [10], [11], [12]. This versatility arises from their foundational training, which equips them with a broad, generalized understanding and the ability to capture diverse knowledge across multiple domains. 

Fine-tuning involves updating a model’s parameters by training it on a dataset specific to a given downstream task. In contrast, prompting utilizes the model’s preexisting capabilities directly by providing it with a natural language description or a few examples of the task. Although effective, fine-tuning is resource-intensive, demanding significant computational power for additional training cycles. Furthermore, its applicability can be limited, particularly in scenarios where adequate taskspecific training datasets are unavailable, making prompting a more alternative.

Architecturally, LLMs are typically built upon the transformer framework [13] and can be classified into three primary types: encoder-only, encoder–decoder, and decoder-only. Encoder-only models (e.g., CodeBERT [14], GraphCodeBERT [15], and UniXcoder [16]) are pretrained using a masked language modeling (MLM) objective. Similarly, encoder–decoder models (e.g., PLBART [17], CodeT5 [18], and CodeT5+ [19]) often employ a masked span prediction (MSP) objective. In these training schemes, a small portion of the input tokens (e.g., 15%) is masked, and the model learns to predict the original content. These models are generally pretrained on large-scale code-related data before being fine-tuned for specific downstream tasks, such as defect detection [20, 21], where they achieve superior performance. Decoder-only models, which have also garnered substantial attention, are trained using a causal language modeling (CLM) objective to predict the next token given a sequence of preceding tokens. The GPT series of models [6] are the most representative examples of this architecture and have been instrumental in transitioning LLMs into practical applications. 

Recently, models such as ChatGPT—the successor to InstructGPT [22]—have drawn significant global attention. These models feature a dialog-based interface and are refined using RL with human feedback (RLHF) [22], [23], [24]. The RLHF process begins with supervised fine-tuning of the base model on a small, high-quality dataset of prompt-response pairs. Subsequently, a reward model is trained by sampling several outputs from the fine-tuned model for a given set of prompts; human labelers then rank these outputs according to quality, providing the preference data needed to train the reward model. Finally, a RL algorithm like proximal policy optimization (PPO) [25] is used to optimize the LLM’s parameters, using the reward model to score generated outputs. Through such fine-tuning and alignment with human preferences, LLMs achieve a more nuanced understanding of input prompts and instructions, leading to enhanced performance across a wide array of tasks [22], [26], [27]. 

## _B. RL From Human Feedback_ 

_1) Fundamental Concepts of RL:_ The fundamental idea of RL from human feedback (RLHF) [23] is to enable a model to learn from preference data provided by humans, thereby better understanding and aligning with true human intent and preferences. The RLHF training process can be divided into three steps: collecting human feedback data, training a reward model, and applying a RL algorithm to train a policy, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The human feedback data are primarily generated by the model and then annotated and selected by humans. This section will focus on concepts related to reward model training and RL algorithms. 

The reward model (RM) is a critical component in RLHF algorithms, often architecturally similar to a pretrained language model. It takes a prompt-response pair as input and modifies the model’s output layer to a scalar value head. Although the basic architecture is similar to a language model, instead of predicting a probability distribution over the next token, the

reward model outputs a single numerical score representing the quality or degree of human preference for the input text. The reward model is trained using a special form of supervised learning known as preference learning [29]. The most common method is based on the Bradley-Terry model, which formulates the training objective to maximize the likelihood of preferred responses over dispreferred ones through pairwise comparisons. During training, the model learns to assign higher scores to human-preferred responses while maintaining a margin over less-preferred alternatives. Backpropagation is performed based on the error signal to update the parameters, ultimately yielding a reward function that can simulate human preferences and guide subsequent policy training. 

The RL algorithm then uses the reward model to optimize the target language model. The optimization aims to maximize expected rewards while constraining the policy’s deviation from a reference policy _π_ ref through a KL-divergence penalty term, controlled by a coefficient _β_ . This balance ensures the model pursues higher rewards without drastically departing from reasonable output distributions. The core idea is to have the model continuously adjust its policy to obtain higher quality outputs while maintaining stability during training. In the following sections, we will introduce three RL optimization algorithms that form the basis of this study. 

_2) Proximal Policy Optimization:_ Proximal policy optimization (PPO) [25] is an online RL algorithm proposed by OpenAI in 2017. The term “Proximal” in its name refers to the practice of constraining the degree of difference between the new and old policies during updates, optimizing the target policy under this condition. Traditional policy gradient methods, such as the REINFORCE algorithm [30], suffer from issues like low sample efficiency, difficulty in controlling the update step size, and training instability. To address these problems, researchers developed trust region methods, such as trust region policy optimization (TRPO) [31]. These methods improve stability by limiting the magnitude of policy updates, but TRPO is computationally complex and relatively difficult to implement. The PPO algorithm achieves an effect similar to a trust region by using a clipped objective function, which simplifies implementation while improving computational efficiency. 

The core objective of proximal policy optimization (PPO) is to maximize rewards while constraining policy updates to prevent drastic deviations from previous iterations. This is achieved through a clipping mechanism that bounds the probability ratio between new and old policies, typically within [1 _− ϵ,_ 1 + _ϵ_ ] where _ϵ_ is a small hyperparameter (e.g., 0.2). The clipped objective ensures stable training by limiting how much the policy can change in each update, while still allowing for effective exploration of high-reward responses. The PPO algorithm employs generalized advantage estimation (GAE) to compute advantages, which balances bias and variance in reward signals through a weighted combination of multistep temporal-difference errors. The GAE mechanism introduces two key hyperparameters: a discount factor _γ_ that controls future reward weighting, and a parameter _λ_ that adjusts the bias-variance trade-off. This approach provides more stable gradient estimates compared to single-step advantage calculations, particularly valuable in code generation tasks where delayed rewards are common. 

Unlike the fixed reference policy used in standard RLHF, PPO maintains a dynamic “old policy” snapshot that is periodically updated. During training, the current policy parameters _θ_ are duplicated as _θ_ old at the start of each update cycle. This snapshot policy is then used to generate multiple batches of data for stable training, implementing an implicit trust region through: 1) importance sampling using the probability ratio _πθ_ ( _y|x_ ) _/πθ_ old ( _y|x_ ) as the weighting factor; and 2) clipping these ratios to prevent overly aggressive policy updates. This dual mechanism—combining GAE for advantage estimation with constrained policy updates—gives PPO its characteristic balance between sample efficiency and training stability, making it particularly suitable for LLM fine-tuning. 

_3) Direct Preference Optimization:_ Direct preference optimization (DPO) [32] is an offline RL algorithm that simplifies the RLHF pipeline by eliminating the need for separate reward modeling. Instead of training a reward model, DPO directly optimizes the policy using preference data, reformulating the RL objective as a binary classification task that distinguishes preferred responses from dispreferred ones. The key innovation lies in its implicit reward modeling through policy likelihood ratios, where the optimization maximizes the relative log-probability margin between preferred ( _yw_ ) and dispreferred ( _yl_ ) outputs while constraining deviations from a reference policy _π_ ref via a coefficient _β_ . This approach maintains the benefits of preference alignment while avoiding the computational overhead and potential instability of explicit reward modeling. 

Recent work has explored applying DPO specifically to optimize code generation models for correctness and efficiency. CodeDPO [33] proposes an offline framework that constructs preference datasets through a self-generation-and-validation mechanism. It leverages a pagerank-inspired algorithm to iteratively score generated code snippets and test cases based on mutual pass rates, aiming to identify more reliable correct solutions. For efficiency optimization, CodeDPO measures execution time on credible test subsets to construct preference pairs favoring faster implementations. While demonstrating effectiveness in boosting correctness and efficiency metrics on standard benchmarks, CodeDPO operates within the offline DPO paradigm, relying on a preconstructed static dataset. 

In contrast, our work (DC-GRPO) addresses the distinct challenge of entropy collapse encountered during _online_ RL fine-tuning for code efficiency optimization. We focus on dynamically adjusting the learning process itself within an online loop to promote exploration of high-reward, low-probability efficient solutions, utilizing a multidimensional reward function that directly incorporates real-time execution measurements. This fundamental difference in paradigm enables DC-GRPO to adaptively explore the solution space beyond the static boundaries of precollected preference data. 

The theoretical foundation of DPO stems from establishing a direct mapping between reward functions and optimal policies under the Bradley-Terry preference model assumption. This derivation reveals how the implicit reward modeling in DPO bypasses explicit reward function estimation while maintaining alignment with human preferences. The key insight shows that the optimal policy for standard RL objectives naturally takes a form where rewards are expressible through log-probability ratios between the learned policy and a reference policy, scaled by a temperature parameter _β_ . 

Crucially, the partition function _Z_ ( _x_ )—which normalizes the policy distribution—becomes a constant offset that cancels out during gradient computation. This elegant mathematical property allows DPO to transform the original RL problem into a direct classification objective comparing preferred and dispreferred outputs. The final DPO loss emerges from substituting this reward-policy relationship back into the Bradley-Terry model, yielding a practical optimization target that requires only policy likelihood evaluations rather than reward modeling. 

This theoretical framework explains DPO’s effectiveness: it preserves the reward maximization principle of RL while eliminating unstable components such as reward function approximation. For code generation tasks, the derivation guarantees that optimizing the DPO objective simultaneously improves both correctness (through the preference model) and efficiency (via appropriate dataset construction), though limited by its offline nature as discussed in our comparison with DC-GRPO. 

Unlike the PPO algorithm which generates data online, DPO training requires a preprepared preference dataset, where each entry typically consists of a query, a preferred response, and a dispreferred response. Training proceeds by minimizing the loss function to update the policy via backpropagation, ultimately completing the model’s training.

## III. DYNAMIC CLIP GROUP RELATIVE POLICY OPTIMIZATION 

_4) Group Relative Policy Optimization:_ Group relative policy optimization (GRPO) [34] is an online RL algorithm proposed by the DeepSeek team. As mentioned in Section II-B2, the PPO algorithm needs to update both a policy and a value network, which incurs significant memory and computational costs. Furthermore, the training of the value network itself can suffer from underfitting or overfitting, which can substantially impact the final model’s performance. The core innovation of the GRPO algorithm was proposed to address these issues, aiming to solve the efficiency and stability problems of PPO in LLM training, making it particularly suitable for scenarios with limited computational resources or where efficient training is required. NFT [35] incorporates negative feedback into SFT by modeling implicit negative policies, improving exploration. Yet, it lacks explicit reward signals for code execution efficiency. 

Compared to PPO, the GRPO algorithm does not require training a value network, as shown in Fig. 3. For a given query, it generates multiple different responses and calculates the advantage by comparing the relative reward scores among these responses, thereby greatly reducing computational and memory resource consumption. 

Group relative policy optimization (GRPO) introduces two key innovations over traditional PPO for efficient language model alignment. First, it eliminates the need for a separate value network by computing relative advantages through intragroup response comparisons. For each query, the algorithm generates multiple candidate responses ( _G_ in total) and evaluates them using a shared reward model. The advantages are normalized within each group to stabilize training signals, either through result-based supervision (comparing final outputs) or process-based supervision (evaluating generation steps). 

Second, GRPO incorporates a novel unbiased KL-divergence estimator directly into its objective function, replacing the standard KL penalty. This estimator dynamically measures the divergence between the current policy _πθ_ and reference policy _π_ ref during token-level updates, constrained by a coefficient _β_ . The complete optimization combines: a) clipped probability ratios between current and old policies (bounded by _ϵ_ to prevent over-updating); b) group-normalized advantages; and c) the KL constraint—all computed efficiently without value function approximation. 

The fixed pruning mechanism of GRPO can easily lead to the problem of “entropy collapse,” which may cause the model to fall into local optima. To address this, we propose the DCGRPO algorithm: a multilevel reward function is designed for compilation pass rate, functional correctness, and execution efficiency to alleviate the sparse reward problem. This section introduces the dynamic clip group relative policy optimization (DC-GRPO) algorithm, covering its overall framework and the specific design of its components. The complete algorithmic framework is illustrated in Fig. 4. 

The DC-GRPO algorithm consists of four main steps. 

- a) A pretrained large code model generates a set of G responses for a given problem. 

- b) A reward model or function is designed to evaluate all generated responses and calculate the intragroup relative advantage for each response. 

- c) The clipping threshold is dynamically adjusted based on the response’s advantage value and generation probability. 

- d) The value of the optimization objective is calculated, and the model’s parameters are updated. 

Among these, the reward function design and the implementation of the dynamic clipping mechanism are the core components of the algorithm and are therefore detailed in the following sections. 

## _A. Reward Function Design_ 

The core idea of RL is to optimize a model’s behavior by maximizing a reward score. The reward function is a quantitative representation of environmental feedback. It directly defines the objective of the RL process. Its quality also affects the reliability of the intragroup relative advantage calculation. If the reward function does not accurately reflect differences in code execution efficiency, GRPO’s relative comparison mechanism may yield misleading policy gradients. This can trap the optimization process in local optima. Therefore, careful reward design is crucial for the method’s effectiveness. In this article, we design a multilevel composite reward function. It guides the model toward high-efficiency code through the synergistic action of three dimensions: compilation reward, correctness reward, and efficiency reward.

A successful compilation yields a reward of 1; otherwise, the reward is 0. This design is practical for code generation. Successful compilation is a prerequisite for executability, so the signal is inherently dense. The model thus receives frequent positive feedback. This reduces the sparse-reward problem common in RL. When computing the intragroup relative advantage, the discrete compilation reward complements the continuous efficiency reward. Together, they provide a stable path for policy optimization. 

_2) Correctness Reward:_ Code that compiles is not necessarily usable. Functional correctness is a prerequisite for usability. The correctness reward directly quantifies the functional completeness of the generated code. It ensures that outputs adhere to the problem specification and avoids invalid code with logical errors or missing functionality. A purely binary pass/fail signal cannot capture partial progress. In contrast, the correctness reward provides informative feedback even when only some test cases pass. This also mitigates low exploration efficiency caused by sparse rewards. We keep the definition of efficiency consistent with the previous chapter. If a program passes all test cases, its efficiency eff is the total execution time over all test cases. If it fails any test case, its efficiency is defined as + _∞_ . The computation proceeds in three steps: a) compute eff for every candidate using the above rule; if eff = + _∞_ , replace it with the maximum finite execution time among all candidates; b) normalize all efficiency values to the interval [0 _,_ 1]; and c) subtract the normalized value from 1 to obtain the final efficiency score. 

Several details require clarification. 

- a) _Why set_ eff = + _∞ for failing code?_ We sum execution time over all test cases. Summing is unfair for code that fails many tests, because its partial timing cannot be compared directly with code that completes all tests. Using average time would be misleading. For example, suppose code A passes all tests and code B passes only the simplest test. Averaging can make B appear faster, even though it fails the difficult and time-consuming cases. Assigning + _∞_ avoids this issue. It does not make such code useless, because the correctness reward still differentiates it within the group.

If a candidate has finite efficiency, it must have correctness reward 1; therefore, its total reward exceeds that of any candidate with + _∞_ efficiency. Subtracting the normalized value from 1 enforces the principle that longer runtime yields a lower score.

It enforces adherence to syntax rules (e.g., preventing missing semicolons or type errors) and filters out invalid snippets. Its binary nature, however, limits information content. We thus assign it the lowest weight (0.5) to avoid overpowering higher-level objectives. The correctness reward promotes functional validity, which is central to efficiency optimization. We assign it a standard weight (1.0). The efficiency reward then guides the model—once correctness is achieved-toward humanpreferred faster implementations. We assign it a higher weight (1.5)

## _B. Dynamic Clipping Mechanism_ 

The previous section introduced the design of the fused reward function. However, the reward value does not directly participate in the calculation of the optimization objective; first, the intragroup relative advantage for each response must be computed. The process is as follows: first, calculate the reward score _ri_ for each output _oi_ . Then, for all responses to the same problem, compute the standardized score _r_ ˜ _i_ = ( _ri −_ mean( _r_ ) _/_ std( _r_ )). The advantage of a response is defined as _A_<sup>ˆ</sup> _i,t_ = ˜ _ri_ . The intragroup relative advantage, derived from the reward scores, more intuitively reflects the quality ranking among responses within a group. As seen from the formula, the advantage value can be positive or negative depending on whether the reward is above or below the mean, which more effectively guides the model’s updates in the optimization objective. In this work, we define a positive advantage value as “high-reward” and a negative value as “low-reward.” 

As analyzed earlier, with a standard fixed clipping coefficient (e.g., 0.2), a low-probability response struggles to increase its probability even with a high reward, while a high-probability response struggles to decrease its probability even with a low reward. This adversely affects model training. Intuitively, highreward responses are more desirable and should be given a higher clipping upper bound. Concurrently, the research by Yu et al. [4] has shown that appropriately increasing the clipping upper bound can, to some extent, alleviate entropy collapse and give low-probability responses a chance to be promoted. Symmetrically, if a high-probability response has a low reward, it does not align with human preferences and should be assigned a lower clipping lower bound to more significantly reduce its probability. Based on this analysis, we propose a dynamic clipping mechanism.

## IV. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 

## _A. Dataset_ 

The foundational dataset used in this study is the APPS dataset [36], a pioneering benchmark for code generation. It is designed to provide a comprehensive and rigorous evaluation framework for the programming capabilities of LLMs. The dataset simulates real-world programming challenges, focusing on a model’s ability to generate Python code from natural language descriptions. Its core design philosophy is derived from the practical methods used in industry to assess software developers, namely, verifying code correctness through openended problem descriptions and automated test cases, rather than relying on ambiguous text similarity metrics. 

APPS contains 10 000 unique programming problems, spanning a wide range of difficulties from basic syntax exercises to highly challenging algorithmic problems at the level of the international olympiad in informatics (IOI). These problems are sourced from the public problem sets of renowned competitive programming platforms such as Codeforces, Kattis, AtCoder, and LeetCode, and have undergone a rigorous process of deduplication, format standardization, and content validation. Each problem includes a complete natural language description (with an average length of 293.2 words) and a total of 131 777 carefully designed test cases (averaging 21.2 test cases per problem in the test set). These test cases cover boundary conditions, edge cases, and functional completeness, ensuring the comprehensiveness of the evaluation. The problems in APPS are divided into three difficulty levels. 

- 1) _Introductory_ : Aimed at developers with 1–2 years of experience, these problems focus on basic syntax and simple logic implementation (e.g., string manipulation, array summation). A typical problem is “count the number of vowels in a string.” 

- 2) _Interview:_ Simulating technical interview scenarios, these problems require the application of data structures (e.g., trees, graphs) and classic algorithms (e.g., dynamic programming, search). An example is “implement the lowest common ancestor algorithm for a binary tree.” 

- 3) _Competition:_ Comparable to professional contests like the ACM/ICPC, these problems involve complex mathematical modeling and optimization strategies (e.g., a combinatorial counting problem like “calculate the number of array pairs that satisfy nondecreasing/ nonincreasing conditions”).

_Case Study._ To provide a more intuitive understanding of how DC-GRPO fine-tuning improves the efficiency of code generated by large models, this section presents a comparative analysis of two code snippets. The problem can be briefly described as: for _T_ given input lines, each with a range [ _l, r_ ], output two integers, _x_ and _y_ , such that _x_ divides _y_ , and both _x_ and _y_ are within that range. The input consists of the number of lines _T_ , followed by _T_ lines each containing two integers that define the output boundaries. 

Comparing the two code snippets, we can see that the first one processes and prints the output directly during input reading. It uses the lower bound ‘l’ as the first number and ‘l _∗_ 2’ as the second, which not only ensures that the former divides the latter but also vastly improves efficiency. The second snippet, however, generates code that strictly follows the problem description. It first defines a function to find an answer based on the boundaries, then methodically reads all inputs, builds an array of queries, and finally iterates multiple times to find the answers. This approach is clearly less efficient than the first. The DC-GRPO method, by generating several code samples during training, can identify such contrasts between efficient and inefficient solutions. By learning from the intragroup relative advantage values, the model learns to generate highly efficient code like the first snippet, rather than merely satisfying the problem description literally.

## _E. Ablation Study_ 

As shown in (5), the clipping weight _δi_ is calculated from both probability and advantage components. This section investigates the impact of these two modules within the dynamic clipping mechanism on the overall performance of the method. Based on the analysis in the previous section, the clipping adjustment magnitude _λ_ is set to 0.15. The results are presented in Table X. In the table, “DC-GRPO w/o _π_ ” denotes a variant that only uses the advantage for adjustment [by setting the probability-based term in (5) to (1)], and “DC-GRPO w/o A” denotes a variant that only uses the probability for adjustment (by setting the advantage-based term to 1). 

The data in the table shows that the complete DC-GRPO method outperforms both “DC-GRPO w/o _π_ ” and “DC-GRPO w/o A” on all metrics, indicating that both the probability weight and the advantage value weight in the clipping adjustment formula are effective and indispensable. This is theoretically intuitive, as adjusting the clipping magnitude based 

solely on probability or solely on reward is one-sided. For example, consider two high-reward responses with different positive advantage values. If they have the same low probability, the “DC-GRPO w/o A” method would assign them the same clipping upper bound. However, common sense dictates that the response with the higher advantage is more desirable and should be given a higher ceiling. Conversely, for the “DCGRPO w/o _π_ ” method, adjusting only based on advantage might result in a relatively high clipping lower bound for a highprobability response whose advantage is negative but not very large. From a human preference standpoint, we would want the model to rapidly decrease the generation probability of such high-probability, low-reward responses, but “DC-GRPO w/o _π_ ” lacks the probability information to make a larger adjustment. 

Additionally, both “DC-GRPO w/o _π_ ” and “DC-GRPO w/o A” show an overall improvement compared to standard GRPO. This is because in the clipping magnitude calculation, any positive advantage will raise the upper bound, and any negative advantage will lower the lower bound. This means that even when considering only one of the two factors, the general direction of the adjustment remains correct; only the precision of the adjustment is inferior to that of the complete DC-GRPO method. 

Based on these results and the analysis, we can conclude that it is essential to consider both the advantage value and the generation probability of a response when adjusting the clipping magnitude. Both components jointly contribute to the effectiveness of the DC-GRPO method. 

## VI. CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION 

This article introduced dynamic clip group relative policy optimization (DC-GRPO), a novel RL framework to enhance the execution efficiency of code generated by LLMs. We identified that the fixed clipping in standard GRPO can lead to premature convergence and suboptimal solutions due to entropy collapse. To overcome this, DC-GRPO introduces a dynamic clipping mechanism. This core innovation adjusts exploration based on the advantage and probability of generated responses,

Complemented by a multicomponent reward function prioritizing execution efficiency, our approach steers the model towards human-preferred, high-performance solutions. Experiments on the APPS dataset validate that DC-GRPO significantly improves the performance of large code models compared to established baselines. 

Our approach complements, rather than replaces, existing efficiency-focused frameworks. Compared to CodeDPO-an _offline_ preference optimization paradigm that requires explicitly constructed preference pairs of positive/negative examples-DC-GRPO operates in an _online_ RL setting: it requires only problem prompts and uses a multiobjective reward (compilation/correctness/efficiency) to update the policy without labeled preference pairs. Methodologically, CodeDPO aligns policies to a static preference dataset, whereas DC-GRPO dynamically _explores_ high-reward, low-probability solutions via an advantage-probability based dynamic clipping scheme that mitigates entropy collapse during training. 

On the evaluation side, eff@k-style metrics and efficiency benchmarks are orthogonal to our training algorithm. We adopt _efficiency-aware metrics_ on APPS to measure whether at least one of the top- _k_ samples is both correct and faster than a reference; by design, these metrics are _evaluation protocols_ , not training procedures. They can be paired with our algorithm or with alternative methods such as CodeDPO. Recent work like COFFE proposes an efficiency benchmark and the metric _efficient@k_ based on CPU-instruction counts for stability; such benchmarks provide _testing grounds_ , whereas DC-GRPO contributes a _learning mechanism_ for improving efficiency under online RL.
