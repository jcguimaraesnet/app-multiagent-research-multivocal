# **Can generative AI bridge the gap? A quasi-experimental study of non-programmers with AI vs. programmers without AI**

Received: 8 September 2025 / Accepted: 21 January 2026 / Published online: 12 February 2026 © The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Science+Business Media, LLC, part of Springer Nature 2026 

### **Abstract** 

In a context where it is debated whether learning to program will remain necessary or whether artificial intelligence (AI) can perform programming on behalf of users, this article reports an _exploratory empirical study_ asking whether individuals without prior programming experience can effectively solve simple coding tasks using a generative tool with a natural-language interface. We used ChatGPT (GPT-4o) because its conversational interface is accessible to non-programmers, unlike tools such as Copilot or Codex that require IDE integration and greater technical setup. We implemented a quasi-experimental design comparing two plausible strategies for solving the same tasks, and analyzed task success using a **_χ_**<sup>**2**</sup> test of independence with effect sizes, and resolution time using Welch’s **_t_** -tests complemented by non-parametric Mann–Whitney **_U_** tests as robustness checks. The findings suggest that, in this controlled setting, a conversational code-generation tool can enable some non-programmers to solve small, well-specified programming challenges with effectiveness comparable to programmers without AI, and with substantially shorter completion times, especially for the high-complexity task. However, we do not claim that AI fully bridges the gap between experts and non-experts: our design does not include a programmers-with-AI condition nor a non-programmers-without-AI baseline, and focuses on short, individual tasks rather than sustained software development. We therefore interpret our results as exploratory evidence of AI as an enabler for non-programmers under constrained conditions, which complements existing work on AI-assisted programming and informs ongoing debates on how AI may reshape roles in software engineering.

## **1 Introduction** 

The rise of generative artificial intelligence (AI) has intensified a central question across both education and professional practice: will learning to program remain essential, or will AI be able to perform programming on behalf of humans? This issue is not only technical; it also involves educational, economic, and organizational dimensions. A recent synthesis situates this debate within a growing body of evidence: large language models (LLMs) can assist a broad range of software engineering (SE) tasks yet exhibit non-trivial limitations and risks that warrant oversight (Zheng et al. 2025). 

On one hand, it has been argued that these tools can remove the barrier of coding, democratizing access to software development (Ko et al. 2011; Ajimati et al. 2025). On the other hand, several authors contend that rather than replacing programmers, AI will transform their roles and competencies (Barke et al. 2023; Hao et al. 2024). 

Software development is a complex process that typically demands significant time and effort. Learners in this discipline invest years becoming familiar with diverse techniques, methodologies, and tools. Mastering one or more programming languages is not sufficient, since coding is only one of the tasks within the broader process. Understanding a problem, expressing it in terms of inputs and processes leading to outputs, implementing these processes through one or more programs, verifying that the solution works correctly, and maintaining it over time are the essence of software development. Broadly, it encompasses stages such as requirements management, analysis, design, coding, verification, and maintenance (Blanco-Llano and Rodríguez-Hernández 2011). 

Requirements engineering—covering the elicitation, analysis, and formalization of user needs—is particularly critical, as it ensures that software meets expectations accurately and prevents costly errors in later stages (Nuseibeh and Easterbrook 2000; Zave and Jackson 1997). From that understanding and formalization, development proceeds to coding. This task involves writing a program in one or more programming languages so that a computer can execute the solution. However, this stage can partly be regarded as rework: the prior understanding is already expressed in natural language, often supported by sketches or diagrams. It is precisely this translation effort that generative AI tools aim to reduce or at least facilitate. The emergence of LLMs, capable of processing natural language, images, and other formats, as well as generating source code in multiple languages, has made partially automated coding possible. 

In parallel, low-code and no-code platforms have transformed software development by enabling people with minimal coding experience to build applications rapidly through visual interfaces, drag-and-drop components, and prebuilt templates (Ajimati et al. 2025). These platforms democratize application development by empowering so-called “citizen developers.” The integration of generative AI further deepens this trend, automating parts of code writing, design, and optimization, and simplifying the assembly of complete solutions. 

Recent surveys confirm this transition: Over 80% of developers report occasionally or regularly using AI assistants across development activities (Sergeyuk et al. 2025; Stack Overflow 2025). At the same time, doubts persist regarding the accuracy of generated solutions and their capacity to handle complex tasks. Recent experimental evidence provides nuance: Ziegler et al. (2024) show that GitHub Copilot significantly reduces completion time on standardized tasks, and report positive effects of Copilot in a field experiment with professional programmers. Complementing these findings, recent work reports domain-

## **2 Background and Related Work** 

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has begun to transform software engineering, introducing tools that assist or, in some cases, partially automate coding tasks. LLM-based tools such as GitHub Copilot and similar code assistants are rapidly becoming valuable assets for professional developers, supporting activities such as code completion, exploration of alternatives, and boilerplate generation (Barke et al. 2023; Ziegler et al. 2024; Murali et al. 2024). Our study is situated in this broader landscape and examines to what extent a general-purpose conversational LLM can help non-programmers solve simple programming tasks, in a controlled comparison against programmers working without AI (the P-noAI condition). Rather than assuming that AI fully closes the skill gap, we treat this comparison as a boundary case that illustrates how far non-programmers can get under favourable conditions with AI support. 

### **2.1 Evaluating Code LLMs: Functional Correctness, Security, and Quality** 

Empirical research on code-oriented LLMs has traditionally focused on measuring their ability to produce functionally correct code. Text-based similarity metrics are widely recognized as insufficient to capture functional equivalence, so the de facto standard is to evaluate whether generated programs pass a predefined suite of unit or acceptance tests. This evaluation paradigm was popularized by Chen et al.’s work on Codex, which assessed LLMs on collections of programming challenges with difficulty comparable to simple interview problems (Chen et al. 2021). In line with this practice, our experimental tasks are short, selfcontained challenges with well-defined acceptance tests, enabling automatic, reproducible assessment of success. 

Functional correctness, however, is only one dimension of interest. Recent work has highlighted that code produced by LLMs can introduce security vulnerabilities and other quality issues. For example, Tihanyi et al. systematically compared several state-of-the-art LLMs, including GPT-4o-mini and Gemini Pro, and identified and quantified security vulnerabilities in their generated code (Tihanyi et al. 2025). In parallel, research on self-admitted technical debt (SATD) has shown that comments documenting shortcuts and deferred work are an important maintainability concern. Sheikhaei et al. demonstrate that LLMs can be effective at detecting and classifying SATD in code bases (Sheikhaei et al. 2024), suggesting that the quality of AI-generated code—and the debt it may implicitly accumulate— is an active topic of investigation. 

Taken together, these findings indicate that, although LLMs are increasingly competent at producing code that passes tests, human inspection and validation remain crucial, especially when security and long-term maintainability are at stake. Our study focuses on functional success and completion time as primary outcomes, but we explicitly acknowledge that quality, security, and maintainability are essential complementary dimensions, which we address in a separate companion study.

### **2.2 Novices and Non-programmers Interacting with AI** 

A substantial body of empirical work has documented the positive impact of AI assistance on the productivity of experienced programmers. Studies in industrial and professional contexts report that developers use tools such as Copilot and ChatGPT to accelerate routine coding, explore design alternatives, and offload boilerplate work (Barke et al. 2023; Ziegler et al. 2024; Murali et al. 2024; Hao et al. 2024). These studies typically assume that users already possess programming expertise and examine how AI integrates into existing workflows and toolchains. 

In parallel, the rapid adoption of LLMs has triggered intense debate in computing education. The SIGCSE working group report “The Robots are Here” provides a comprehensive overview of pedagogical implications, threats to academic integrity, and the need for rigorous empirical research on how generative AI affects learning and assessment in computer science (Prather et al. 2023). Empirical studies have begun to investigate how novices and students use code-oriented LLMs for solving, explaining, and debugging code (Denny et al. 2024; Kazemitabaar et al. 2023). For example, Kazemitabaar et al. show that AI code generators can help learners aged 10–17 obtain functionally correct solutions and overcome initial barriers, without substantially harming performance on subsequent manual code modification tasks. These findings support the idea that LLMs can act as scaffolding for novices, helping them get started on programming problems that would otherwise be out of reach. 

At the same time, the success of non-programmers and novices depends critically on their ability to communicate effectively with the model in natural language. The literature highlights several challenges around specification and prompting. Jayagopal et al. studied novices using program synthesizers (rather than conversational LLMs) and found that they often struggled to decide which details to provide and what language to use in their specifications, leading to frequent synthesis failures (Jayagopal et al. 2022). Feldman and Anderson report that non-experts interacting with AI tools frequently grapple with vagueness and specificity in their prompts, sometimes assuming that the model understands intentions and synonyms that, in practice, require precise technical wording (Feldman and Anderson 2024). Nguyen et al. similarly observe that code LLMs, trained predominantly on expert code, tend to “expect” prompts that incorporate programming terminology, creating a misalignment between how novices naturally describe problems and how the models interpret them (Nguyen et al. 2024). 

The interaction modality also matters. Using a chat-style conversational interface such as ChatGPT (GPT-4o) is a methodological choice aimed at ecological validity, as many developers and non-developers now interact with LLMs via conversational tools rather than IDE plug-ins alone. Hao et al. analyzed real-world conversations that developers shared when using ChatGPT on GitHub, and found that professionals rely heavily on multi-turn strategies, including iterative follow-ups to refine prompts and explicit requests for clarification (Hao et al. 2024). This suggests that the ability to “converse” with the model—to refine, reformulate, and negotiate problem statements over multiple turns—is a central skill even for experienced developers, and likely even more critical for non-programmers. Our study builds on this insight by allowing non-programmers in the NP-AI group to freely engage in

### **2.3 Methodological Justification and Empirical Standards** 

Because our goal is to establish a controlled comparison between two realistic strategies for solving the same programming tasks, it is important to ground our methodology in established frameworks for quasi-experimental research and empirical software engineering. We follow Shadish et al.’s framework for generalizing causal inferences from quasi-experiments (Shadish et al. 2002), explicitly considering threats to internal, construct, external, and statistical conclusion validity. Given that the effects of LLMs are highly context dependent, we interpret our results as exploratory evidence within a narrow, well-defined scenario rather than as universal claims. 

The design and reporting of our study are also informed by guidelines for experiments in software engineering (Jedlitschka et al. 2008; Wohlin et al. 2012). We specify our a priori power analysis for the primary outcome (task success), clearly distinguish primary analyses from exploratory and robustness checks in Section 3.2, and provide a replication package including the dataset, analysis notebook, and exported tables. 

From a statistical standpoint, we follow long-standing recommendations to report effect sizes alongside _p_ -values (Cohen 1988). In addition to traditional parametric measures (e.g., Hedges’ _g_ for time comparisons), we use non-parametric effect sizes such as Cliff’s _δ_ and Vargha–Delaney’s _A_ 12, computed with established tools (Macbeth et al. 2011). This is particularly important given our modest sample size and the skewed distributions typical of time measurements. In interpreting non-significant findings, we adopt the perspective advocated by Zheng et al. for empirical studies of LLM-supported software engineering: with limited power to detect small effects, failure to reject the null should not be interpreted as evidence of absence of an effect (Zheng et al. 2025). Instead, we focus on the size and direction of estimated effects and treat many subgroup and complexity-wise analyses as exploratory, to be confirmed or refuted in larger replications. 

Overall, our focus on short, self-contained programming tasks, a conversational LLM interface, and explicit effect size reporting reflects a methodological choice aimed at combining ecological realism with statistical rigor under the constraints of a quasi-experimental design.

## **3 Method** 

This section describes in detail the quasi-experimental design, the statistical analyses conducted, the characteristics of the participants, the materials and instruments employed, the protocol followed, and the ethical considerations of the study, following reporting recommendations for software engineering experiments (Jedlitschka et al. 2008). Analytical choices (non-parametric tests, effect sizes, and multiple-comparison adjustments) are made to enhance robustness and interpretability in line with common practice in software engineering experiments. 

### **3.1 Design** 

A quasi-experimental non-equivalent group design was employed (Shadish et al. 2002), in which AI use was nested within the non-programmer group. Although this choice precludes isolating the effect of AI from participant profile, a full 2 _×_ 2 factorial design (programmers vs. non-programmers _×_ with vs. without AI) was not viable for this pilot study: a group of non-programmers without AI would have exhibited a predictably null performance, and programmers with AI have already been studied in prior work (Ziegler et al. 2024). 

Consequently, the design focused on comparing two practical and contrasting strategies: non-programmers empowered by AI versus programmers working without assistance. The goal was to assess whether AI narrows the performance gap on simple programming tasks under realistic, controlled conditions. 

The experiment was thus structured into two main groups of participants, defined by their programming experience, which also determined AI usage. Group 1, non-programmers with AI (NP-AI), comprised individuals with no prior programming knowledge, assisted by ChatGPT (GPT-4o). Group 2, programmers without AI (P-noAI), included individuals with programming knowledge, equally divided between those with basic experience (novices) and those with extensive experience (experts), without access to AI-based code generation tools. 

The assigned task consisted of solving one of three programming challenges of increasing complexity, based on everyday problems. Challenge assignment was randomized, aiming for balanced distribution across difficulty levels and groups. Instructions were kept homogeneous within each group, and experimenter intervention was minimized to reduce bias. 

It is important to stress that the purpose of this study is not to claim that AI fully replaces programming expertise or eliminates the need for professional developers. Rather, we treat our comparison as a boundary case: it explores how far non-programmers can get on small, well-specified programming challenges when assisted by a conversational AI tool, relative to a baseline of programmers (novices and experts) working without AI. The design does not include a programmers-with-AI condition nor a non-programmers-without-AI baseline, and therefore cannot establish whether AI ultimately equalizes performance between experts and non-experts in realistic development settings. 

**Notation** We denote the AI-assisted non-programmer group as NP-AI and the programmers without AI as P-noAI; these labels are used consistently throughout.

### **3.2 Data Analysis** 

We analyzed two primary outcomes: _effectiveness_ (task success: all acceptance tests passed vs. not) and _resolution time_ (minutes from task assignment to final submission). Our analysis plan distinguished between primary confirmatory tests and exploratory or robustness checks. 

**Primary Analyses** For effectiveness, we compared the proportion of successful participants between NP-AI and P-noAI using a _χ_<sup>2</sup> test of independence with one degree of freedom, reporting the _χ_<sup>2</sup> statistic, _p_ -value, and associated effect sizes (Cramér’s _V_ and odds ratios with confidence intervals). This test and its corresponding power analysis (Section 3.3) were defined a priori for the aggregated comparison between NP-AI and P-noAI. 

For resolution time, we compared groups using Welch’s _t_ -tests, which are robust to unequal variances. Given the positive skew of raw times, we analyzed both untransformed times and log(time); the conclusions were consistent, so we report results on the original scale for interpretability, along with Hedges’ _g_ as a standardized effect size. In addition, we ran a twoway ANOVA on log(time) with factors _group_ (NP-AI vs. P-noAI) and _task complexity_ (low, medium, high) to explore whether the time difference varied across complexity levels. 

**Exploratory and Robustness Analyses** All other statistical tests are considered exploratory. First, we performed follow-up comparisons of success rates by task complexity (low, medium, high) using Fisher’s exact tests for each task, given the small cell counts. Second, within P-noAI, we contrasted novices and experts in terms of effectiveness and time using descriptive statistics and non-parametric tests; these analyses were not powered a priori and should be interpreted cautiously. Third, for all between-group time comparisons we complemented Welch’s tests with Mann–Whitney _U_ tests as robustness checks against non-normality. 

To avoid over-emphasizing multiple overlapping metrics, we report in the main text only the most interpretable summaries for each research question (success proportions, odds ratios for success, and Hedges’ _g_ or Cliff’s _δ_ for time), and clearly separate primary analyses from exploratory and robustness checks. The full set of test statistics, _p_ -values, and additional effect size estimates for all exploratory contrasts is available in the replication package and in the online supplementary material. Unless otherwise stated, all tests were two-tailed with _α_ = 0 _._ 05, and _p_ -values for families of exploratory contrasts were adjusted using the Holm procedure. 

### **3.3 Participants** 

To estimate sample size, an a priori power analysis was conducted with G*Power 3.1 (Faul et al. 2009), following methodological standards (Macbeth et al. 2011). Assuming a medium effect ( _w_ = 0 _._ 4), significance level _α_ = 0 _._ 05, power of 0.80, and one degree of freedom, the required minimum was 50 observations. This power analysis explicitly targeted the aggregated comparison between NP-AI and P-noAI in terms of task success using a _χ_<sup>2</sup> test of independence. It was not designed to detect differences between novices and experts within P-noAI or to support separate, fully powered analyses for each task complexity level. Analyses by programmer experience and by complexity are therefore reported as exploratory and underpowered.

## **4 Results** 

We analyzed the collected data in terms of effectiveness and resolution time. The following subsections report these results. 

### **4.1 Effectiveness** 

Effectiveness was analyzed using a _χ_<sup>2</sup> test of independence on a 2 _×_ 2 contingency table contrasting success and failure proportions between groups. The test did not show a significant difference, _χ_<sup>2</sup> (1) = 1 _._ 926, _p_ = 0 _._ 165. The effect size was small ( _φ_ = 0 _._ 193), still below the conventional threshold for a medium effect.. ### **4.4 Summary of Findings** 

Taken together, the results suggest three main conclusions: 

- _Effectiveness:_ success rates were comparable between NP-AI and P-noAI. The _χ_<sup>2</sup> analysis was not significant and the _post hoc_ power was low, which prevents ruling out small between-group effects. This is consistent with a recent synthesis highlighting contextdependent LLM effects and cautioning against overgeneralization from a single design (Zheng et al. 2025). 

- _Efficiency:_ global timing analyses show that NP-AI solved tasks significantly faster than P-noAI. Effect sizes were moderate to large, in line with reports of measurable gains on analysis/QA tasks (Sheikhaei et al. 2024). 

- _Effect of complexity:_ the advantage of NP-AI was amplified in higher-complexity challenges, where statistical significance and large effects were observed. This complements findings that LLM support can better scaffold complex reasoning workflows (Kang et al. 2025). 

The reporting of descriptive statistics, hypothesis tests, and effect sizes follows recommendations for experiments in software engineering (Wohlin et al. 2012). 

## **5 Discussion** 

This study explored two realistic strategies for solving simple programming problems: nonprogrammers using ChatGPT in Spanish and programmers working without AI assistance. Under the specific, controlled conditions of our experiment—small, self-contained tasks with automated tests and a single interaction session—non-programmers with AI support achieved success rates and completion times that were often comparable to, and sometimes better than, those of programmers without AI. We interpret these findings as evidence that generative AI can enable some non-programmers to perform surprisingly well on simple programming problems, not as showing that AI fully “equalizes” skills between non-programmers and professional developers. In realistic settings, programmers also have access to AI tools, and our design does not include either a non-programmers-without-AI baseline or a programmers-with-AI condition. Our claims should therefore be understood as demonstrating the potential of AI as an enabler in a narrow, well-defined scenario, rather than as evidence that AI eliminates expertise gaps. 

Our study complements prior work on AI-assisted programming for professional developers (e.g., Barke et al. 2023; Ziegler et al. 2024; Sheikhaei et al. 2024), on novices and students using code LLMs in educational settings (e.g., Denny et al. 2024; Prather et al. 2023; Kazemitabaar et al. 2023), and on non-programmers and end users interacting with AI to specify or generate code (e.g., Feldman and Anderson 2024; Jayagopal et al. 2022; Pickering et al. 2025). While these lines of research examine (i) professional developers’ productivity, (ii) learners’ use of LLMs in CS education, or (iii) how non-programmers communicate requirements to AI tools, our quasi-experiment explicitly contrasts non-programmers with AI and programmers without AI on the same set of tasks. This provides a complementary perspective on AI as an enabler for new participants in certain programming activities.

The most salient quantitative difference in our results emerged in resolution times. When considering all participants, NP-AI completed the tasks substantially faster than P-noAI (median 49.5 vs. 95 minutes), with a small-to-medium effect size. Among those who produced successful solutions, time differences were much smaller and statistically uncertain, which suggests that the global time advantage of NP-AI is largely driven by the higher proportion of successful participants in that group. The breakdown by task complexity reinforces this interpretation. For the medium-complexity task, NP-AI participants tended to finish somewhat faster, but the evidence for a difference was weak and did not survive Holm correction. As recommended in recent discussions on statistical practice in empirical software engineering (e.g., Zheng et al. 2025), we therefore treat these complexity-wise comparisons as hypothesis-generating rather than as confirmatory evidence. They indicate where AI assistance might be especially beneficial, but they require confirmation in larger, pre-registered replications. 

Within P-noAI, experts were somewhat more likely to succeed and tended to complete the task faster than novices (46.2% vs. 30.8% success; mean times 85.54 vs. 112.69 minutes, respectively). However, they are based on only 13 participants per subgroup and were not part of our a priori power analysis. We therefore treated all analyses by programmer experience as exploratory, using them primarily to contextualize the aggregated NP-AI vs. P-noAI comparison rather than as the basis for strong claims about the impact of experience. 

From a practical standpoint, our results suggest that AI can enable some non-programmers to engage in limited forms of programming work, at least when the problems are well specified, tests are available, and there is enough time to iterate with the tool. In such contexts, AI may reduce the need for direct involvement of professional developers in some small-scale coding tasks, while simultaneously creating new demands for oversight, integration, and maintenance by experts. 

While these results show that some non-programmers, when equipped with an LLM, can successfully solve simple, unit-testable programming problems, we stop short of claiming that programmer and non-programmer roles are converging. ## **6 Threats to Validity** 

As in any empirical study, several threats should be considered when interpreting the results. We discuss them according to the classic taxonomy of internal, external, construct, and statistical validity. 

### **6.1 Internal Validity** 

The main threat arises from the quasi-experimental design: AI usage was nested in the nonprogrammer group, preventing a clean separation of tool effects from participant profile effects. This decision was deliberate for ecological validity and feasibility: (i) a group of non-programmers without AI would likely exhibit a _predictably failing_ performance, providing only a redundant baseline, and (ii) a group of programmers with AI has already been extensively studied with consistent findings (Ziegler et al. 2024). Extending to a full factorial design would have required a much larger sample with limited additional insight. Our interest was to focus on the unexplored quadrant—non-programmers with AI versus programmers without AI—in controlled yet plausible conditions. Still, we acknowledge that this design does not allow attributing results solely to AI. To mitigate interpretability risks, we triangulated parametric and non-parametric analyses and reported multiple effect sizes. 

An additional risk relates to instructions: non-programmers received specific guidance on how to interact with the AI, while programmers received standard prompts. Although we sought balance, a differential _scaffolding_ effect cannot be ruled out. Finally, awareness of being evaluated may have introduced behavioral biases (Hawthorne effect).

### **6.2 External Validity** 

The study was conducted with a limited sample ( _n_ = 52) of volunteers in a controlled setting, restricting generalization to broader populations and industrial contexts. The tasks, while representative of varying complexity, were narrow in scope and do not capture the richness of real projects. Therefore, the results should be interpreted as exploratory evidence rather than immediately generalizable to professional environments. Consistent with recent syntheses, LLM effects are context-dependent; replications in collaborative, industrial settings are needed to test robustness (Zheng et al. 2025; Murali et al. 2024; Tihanyi et al. 2025). 

Moreover, the two groups differed in age and gender composition: NP-AI participants were on average older and included more women than P-noAI. These differences reflect the distinct recruitment pools (professional and educational contexts), and they may influence how participants perceive and adopt AI tools. As a result, our findings should not be generalized to arbitrary populations of non-programmers and programmers without careful consideration of demographic and contextual factors. 

Additionally, results depend on the chosen generative tool. We used ChatGPT for its accessibility and conversational format suitable for non-programmers; findings may differ with other tools (e.g., GitHub Copilot or Codex) that require IDE integration and prior knowledge. Likewise, our evidence comes from Spanish-language task statements and interactions; results may differ in other languages, and cross-language replications are needed. Moreover, the experimental context was individual and controlled; in industrial settings with collaborative dynamics, reviews, and continuous deployment, AI effects may manifest differently. Security considerations are also salient: prior work reports non-trivial vulnerability rates in AI-generated code, which may affect external validity when moving from sandboxed tasks to production contexts (Tihanyi et al. 2025). 

### **6.3 Construct Validity** 

Dependent variables were measured objectively: effectiveness as passing all test cases, and time as total duration. However, these metrics capture only one dimension of performance. We did not assess code structure quality, maintainability, or subjective experience. Including both successes and failures in the time variable may also introduce bias, as failures typically take longer. Future work should incorporate code-quality and security assessments to capture trade-offs observed in a recent study (Tihanyi et al. 2025). 

Challenge statements were highly specified (goals, business rules, test cases), thereby reducing the ambiguity typical of software development. This decision aimed to equalize conditions and focus the comparison on implementation. Still, it limits generalization to scenarios with incomplete, ambiguous, or evolving requirements—common in practice. Future work should include tasks with greater uncertainty and evaluate code quality and human–AI interactions, dimensions we recorded and can analyze subsequently. This aligns with calls to examine LLM behavior across varied tasks and prompt settings (Zheng et al. 2025). 

Regarding construct validity, our operationalization of performance focuses on task success (all test cases passed) and resolution time. These measures do not fully capture code quality aspects such as structure, readability, maintainability, or security. Nor do they reflect the richness of participants’ interaction with ChatGPT (e.g., number of prompts, types of

### **6.4 Statistical Conclusion Validity** 

The a priori power analysis assumed a medium effect ( _w_ = 0 _._ 40). Observed effects on effectiveness were smaller ( _φ_ = 0 _._ 19), with low _post hoc_ power (1 _− β ≈_ 0 _._ 19), implying limited sensitivity to detect small between-group differences. In other words, the absence of significant differences in effectiveness does not rule out small effects. In contrast, timing results showed moderate-to-large effects (Hedges’ _g_ = _−_ 0 _._ 79 in the global analysis; Cliff’s _δ_ = _−_ 0 _._ 74 at high complexity), reinforcing the robustness of that conclusion. Nevertheless, the sample size remains limited for probing more complex interactions. Interpreting non-significant findings alongside effect sizes and uncertainty is consistent with common practice when sample sizes are modest (Zheng et al. 2025). 

Our statistical conclusion validity is limited by the modest sample size ( _n_ = 52) and by the fact that the a priori power analysis targeted only the aggregated _χ_<sup>2</sup> comparison of success between NP-AI and P-noAI. Subgroup analyses by programmer experience (novice vs. expert) and by task complexity were not powered a priori, and cell counts in some of the corresponding contingency tables are small. We therefore treat these analyses as exploratory and focus our main conclusions on the primary group-level contrasts. 

## **7 Conclusions** 

This quasi-experimental study examined two contrasting strategies for solving simple programming tasks: non-programmers assisted by AI (NP-AI) and programmers without AI (P-noAI). The results showed that success rates were comparable, while resolution times consistently favored NP-AI, particularly in high-complexity challenges. These findings suggest that AI can reduce performance gaps between heterogeneous profiles, enabling individuals without prior programming experience to produce working solutions in controlled and well-defined contexts. This pattern is consistent with recent overviews and task-specific studies reporting context-dependent but tangible efficiency gains with LLM support (Sheikhaei et al. 2024; Zheng et al. 2025; Kang et al. 2025). 

Regarding the motivating question of this work—whether it is still necessary to learn to program in a world where non-programmers can ask AI to write code—our findings offer a nuanced answer. In our quasi-experimental setting, non-programmers assisted by ChatGPT in Spanish achieved success rates comparable to a mixed group of programmers without AI and completed the tasks substantially faster, especially for the most complex challenge. This suggests that generative AI can, under favorable conditions, enable some non-programmers to perform programming tasks that they would otherwise struggle to solve on their own. 

At the same time, our design does not include professional programmers with access to AI tools, nor does it capture longer-term, collaborative, or safety-critical software development scenarios. Existing evidence indicates that programmers also benefit from AI assis-

We thus interpret our results not as proof that AI definitively bridges the gap between experts and non-experts, but as an indication that conversational code-generation tools can broaden who is able to complete certain small-scale programming tasks. Understanding how these tools reshape roles, responsibilities, and required competencies in software engineering remains an important topic for future research. 

These findings should not be interpreted as evidence that programmers can be replaced; rather, they highlight a _complementarity of roles_ . Professional programmers remain indispensable for critical software engineering tasks, while NP-AI may add value in prototyping, automation, and scoped projects. At the organizational and educational level, this points to the challenge of preparing future professionals—both technical and non-technical—to collaborate in AI-mediated hybrid scenarios, developing competencies in problem formulation, critical evaluation, and oversight of AI outputs. Organizations should also establish quality and security guardrails when adopting AI-assisted workflows (Tihanyi et al. 2025). 

For future work, three main directions are identified: (i) enlarging the sample size to increase statistical power and detect small effects; (ii) designing more realistic and collaborative tasks that reflect professional development contexts; and (iii) conducting deeper analyses of code quality and human–AI interaction patterns. These extensions will help strengthen the external and construct validity of the initial findings. Overall, this work should be seen as an _exploratory first step_ contributing initial empirical evidence to the literature on how AI may reconfigure roles in software development. Rather than providing a definitive conclusion, this study sets the stage for a research agenda aimed at deepening our understanding of the scope and limits of collaboration between humans and AI systems in software engineering. Taken together, our results and recent evidence support a view of AI as an amplifier of human capabilities whose benefits and risks vary with task complexity, domain, and the safeguards in place (Sheikhaei et al. 2024; Zheng et al. 2025; Kang et al. 2025; Tihanyi et al. 2025).
